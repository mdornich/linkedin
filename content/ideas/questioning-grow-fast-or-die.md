## Original Prompt
Investors are reaching out to understand what am I doing. And I'm pretty open that I'm not fundraising at the moment, as I prefer to spend my time on the product and talking with customers, and not talking with Investors and then trying to hire people. But I do have the conversations, because who knows, I might make it work and not be able to scale it alone so, at that point I will think about it again. The advice or concern I get mostly is, yeah, but in order to compete you have to raise money and grow fast. But I'm not buying it, as I think it is the investor speaking, more than a reality, because what I'm doing doesn't have such network effects like a social network. Modern QA teams use not only one tool, but many tools to do their job. And in reality the real competition would be around does it solve the right problem and most importantly how well it solves it. There was only one lady I talked with, that gave me valuable advice, which was around differentiation, team (there is no team, it is just me, which is risky for investors) and traction.

## Core Idea
Challenge the blanket "grow fast or die" advice from investors by examining when it actually applies vs. when it's just investor-speak. Use real experience from investor conversations to contrast helpful advice (differentiation, team, traction) with cookie-cutter pressure to raise money and scale quickly.

## Key Points
- Most investor advice is "raise money and grow fast" - but is that reality or just investor interest?
- Network effects matter for social networks, not for all products
- QA teams use multiple tools - the real competition is solving the right problem well
- The difference between helpful investor advice (differentiation, team, traction) and generic scaling pressure
- Solo founder choice: product + customers > fundraising + hiring (for now)
- When keeping options open makes sense without committing to the fundraising treadmill
